A short history of the Polish language

Polish (język polski) is one of the world's major Slavic languages — and a thousand-year story of a tongue that refused to disappear.

Slavic roots

From Proto-Slavic to the Polans

Polish belongs to the West Slavic branch of the Indo-European family, in the Lechitic group alongside Kashubian and the now-extinct Polabian, and closely related to Czech, Slovak, and Sorbian. It descends from Proto-Slavic, the common ancestor that began splitting apart in the early Middle Ages. The language — and the country — take their name from the Polanie ("people of the fields"), a tribe that united the region around Gniezno into an early state.

When Duke Mieszko I accepted Christianity in 966, Latin clergy arrived with the Latin alphabet. For centuries Latin remained the language of church, law, and record, while Polish lived mainly in speech — which is why its earliest written traces are scattered words inside Latin documents.

Old Polish · 10th–16th c.

The first written words

The single most famous fragment appears in the Księga henrykowska (Book of Henryków), a Latin chronicle kept by Cistercian monks in Silesia. Around 1270 the monk Peter recorded a sentence a settler said to his wife as she toiled at the grindstone — and noted in the margin, "Hoc est in polonico" ("this is in Polish"):

"Day, ut ia pobrusa, a ti poziwai"
"Let me grind, and you take a rest." — the oldest sentence written in Polish
Book of Henryków, c. 1270 · UNESCO Memory of the World Register, 2015

Other early monuments followed: the Bogurodzica ("Mother of God"), the oldest Polish religious hymn, later sung by knights before battle; and the Holy Cross Sermons, the oldest surviving Polish prose. Spelling was chaotic — scribes stretched the Latin alphabet to fit Polish sounds it was never built for.

Middle Polish · 16th–18th c.

The Golden Age

The printing press and the Renaissance turned Polish into a full literary language. Mikołaj Rej, often called the father of Polish literature, championed writing in the vernacular, famously insisting that "Poles are not geese, but have a tongue of their own." The poet Jan Kochanowski raised Polish verse to a European standard. This era fixed many of the spelling conventions still used today — the digraphs sz, cz, rz and the diacritic letters that give Polish its bristling look on the page.

Partitions · 1795–1918

Surviving erasure

For 123 years Poland vanished from the map, divided among Russia, Prussia, and Austria. The partitioning powers pushed Russification and Germanization, restricting Polish in schools and offices. Yet the language survived in homes, churches, and a flourishing Romantic literature — Adam Mickiewicz's epic Pan Tadeusz opens with a longing invocation of a homeland that, politically, no longer existed. Polish became the thread that held national identity together until independence returned in 1918.

Modern Polish

The language today

Standard Polish is spoken by around 45 million people and is an official language of Poland and the European Union. It kept features many of its Slavic cousins lost — most strikingly the nasal vowels ą and ę, found in no other major Slavic language. Seven grammatical cases, flexible word order, and consonant clusters (try w Szczebrzeszynie) make it famously challenging — but the spelling, once learned, is highly regular, and stress almost always lands on the second-to-last syllable.

Why this matters in the control room

As Poland builds its first large-scale nuclear power plants, you're joining a language with deep roots and a strong sense of precision. Clear, correct Polish — especially the safety and shift-handover vocabulary in these lessons — isn't just courtesy; in a control room it's part of the safety culture. Powodzenia! (Good luck!)

How to use it

Open a lesson, tap 🔊 to hear each word (your browser's Polish voice), and tap ✓ when you've learned it. Then take the lesson quiz. Your progress saves automatically on this device.

A note on pronunciation

The italic guides are rough English approximations. Polish stress almost always falls on the second-to-last syllable. Use the 🔊 button for the real thing.

Publish it for your team

The app is two files that live side by side: index.html (the app) and words.js (your editable vocabulary bank). To put it online for free with GitHub Pages: create a new public repository, upload both index.html and words.js, then in Settings → Pages set the source to your main branch. Your team gets a public URL like https://yourname.github.io/nuclear-polish/. See How to publish and share.md for the full walkthrough.

Adding your own words

Open words.js in any text editor. It's an alphabetical list with // — A — letter dividers — find the right letter, add a line like ["mostek","footbridge"], and save. The app code never needs to change.
